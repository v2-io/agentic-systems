## Section A — Instructions stress-test

This audit was the first to run the v2 instructions, and I am the same agent that wrote them. The dual nature was acknowledged from the start; the reflections preserve the resulting iteration trail. Three structural observations from the run:

### A.1 The §4.4 cadence directive failed at the most basic level on the v2 (and was strengthened mid-session)

Joseph reported that 0 of 3 agents (me, Codex, Gemini) followed §4.4's per-segment-reflection cadence on first contact. I batched 5 segments in a single parallel Read tool call, then wrote one consolidated reflection. Asked afterward "did you consider writing per-segment files and conclude not to?" the honest answer was *no — the consider never fired as a decision point.* The "consider writing" language doesn't trigger consideration when the agent's default tool-cadence is parallel-read-then-synthesize.

The mid-session strengthening landed three reinforcements: (a) a top-of-file paragraph foregrounding "approximately 50 segments → 40-50 reflections, significantly slower than typical trained workflows"; (b) a §4.4 "Aside" section with explicit historical-honesty about my failure; (c) an "IMPORTANT SELF-CHECK" with five recognition triggers (delegating reading; reading in batches; failing to thoughtfully consider each segment; believing agent summaries; not taking instructions seriously); (d) a "Final Reminder" at the file end stating *"the LLM cannot attend to future segment content when pondering the current segment and updating its understanding."*

**Lesson worth preserving for future instruction work:** soft directives ("consider X") only work when X is in the agent's default option set. If the agent's default tool-cadence is incompatible with X, the consideration step doesn't fire and X is silently skipped. Procedural strictness on the *tool cadence* (one Read per segment) is more effective than procedural strictness on the *reflection format* — the decision point lives at the tool level.

### A.2 The CLAUDE.md / MEMORY.md auto-load priming undermines de-novo posture structurally

The §4.1 directive to avoid TODO.md / PROPOSALS.md before forming first-encounter judgments is *partially undermined* by the project's own auto-load mechanism. CLAUDE.md auto-loads (correct, Claude-Code-native) and currently carries ~70 specific architectural commitments under "What's Settled vs. Open" — equivalent priming density to TODO.md, arguably higher signal-to-noise. MEMORY.md auto-loads with full timeline of recent cycles including F-V findings.

This was addressed mid-session by:
- Splitting CLAUDE.md into CLAUDE.md (kept lighter — orientation, conventions, working posture) and CLAUDE-2.md (deep architectural detail, "What's Settled vs Open", Decision #7's epistemic-architecture seven elements). CLAUDE-2.md gets a header instructing agents not to read it during de-novo audits unless Joseph signals otherwise.
- Migrating bleed-y MEMORY entries (Key Theoretical Developments, Segment Status numbers) to CHANGELOG.md, leaving generic pointers in MEMORY.md.
- Removing Novel Results and Convergent Choices from README.md (moved to CLAUDE-2.md); README's Project Layout updated.

**Lesson worth preserving:** for projects that need fresh-encounter audits, auto-load files are a structural surface that must be designed for the de-novo-audit case, not just the everyday-work case. The split-into-tiers move (load-by-default vs load-when-signaled) is a clean pattern.

### A.3 The OUTLINE-linearized form with backward-dependency-as-finding is an elegant refinement

Mid-session §4.2 was rewritten to: *follow the OUTLINE's linearized rows top-to-bottom; any backward-dependency (a `depends:` slug pointing to something not yet read in OUTLINE order) is a critical finding rather than a navigation problem.* This makes the audit's path deterministic AND turns nonlinearity into framework-level signal rather than agent-level discretion.

The appendix-back-pointer exception was added to handle the legitimate "result-in-body, proof-in-appendix" mathematical-writing convention — appendix-back-pointers from main-section to Appendix-A derivations are explicitly *not* critical findings, and the agent may read the appendix when first referenced. This is the standard mathematical-writing convention; treating it as a critical finding would have produced 7+ false positives on Section I alone (which is what my F-C series captured before being retired).

**Lesson:** strict linearization is a load-bearing discipline for non-appendix backward pointers; the appendix-back-pointer exception preserves the mathematical-writing convention without weakening the discipline.

### A.4 Two §4.4 prompts added mid-session (#12 subjective value; #13 field contribution)

Prompts 1–11 lean toward verification-mode (predictions vs evidence; cross-segment consistency; math verification; etc.). Prompts 12–13 lean toward diffuse / phenomenological / field-reach thinking. Adding them mid-session noticeably enriched the reflections from row 24 onward — surfacing observations that the verification-mode prompts wouldn't have caught (the OKR/AAD operational mapping I found in disc-credit-assignment-boundary, the framework's "feel" calibration that turned out to track engagement, etc.).

**Lesson:** verification prompts and diffuse prompts have different yields. The 11 → 13 expansion is a real instruction improvement.

## Section D — Bigger-picture pondering (Hypothesis-tier)

These are observations worth surfacing at *Hypothesis* level on the epistemic ladder — specific enough to act on, honest about not being verified. Marked clearly so future agents don't mistake them for derived results.

### D.1 Six-mechanism convergence on shallow-plan preference

**Hypothesis.** The framework has *six* independent arguments for shallow plans being structurally superior to deep ones, all multiplicative in depth. They are:

1. **Confidence decay** (`#der-chain-confidence-decay`): aggregate confidence $\prod p_k$ decays geometrically with depth. *Plan less likely to succeed.*
2. **Evidence starvation** (`#deriv-strategic-dynamics`, `#der-observability-dominance`): effective observation rate $\nu_k = \nu \prod_{j<k}\theta_j$ decays. *Plan harder to calibrate.*
3. **Cognitive cost** (`#form-strategy-complexity-cost`): description length grows $O(|E| \log |V|)$. *Plan more expensive to maintain.*
4. **Strategic-tempo bottleneck** (`#def-strategic-tempo`): per-edge persistence requires $\nu_{ij} \cdot \iota_{ij} \cdot \eta_{\text{edge},ij} > \rho_\Sigma/R_\Sigma$, with the deepest edge most fragile.
5. **Identifiability degradation** (`#scope-edge-update-causal-validity`): $\iota_{ij}$ decreases with depth (each level of indirect inference adds attribution uncertainty). *Plan harder to learn causally.*
6. **Interaction-horizon compression** (`#form-strategy-complexity-cost` Discussion, Miller 2022): effective complexity bounded above by interaction-horizon complexity, regardless of agent depth.

**Synthesis observation.** The framework has gradually accumulated five-or-six independent mechanisms each predicting shallow plans win, without (as far as I saw in my reading) consolidating them. `#der-chain-confidence-decay` lists three ("triple depth penalty"); `#scope-edge-update-causal-validity` adds the fourth ($\iota$ decreases with depth); `#def-strategic-tempo` adds the fifth (bottleneck); Miller's interaction-horizon adds the sixth. Each is real; each compounds.

**Possible action.** Elevate the convergence as a meta-observation in either `#disc-separability-pattern` (positive scope-half) or as its own subsection. The structural significance is that AAD's recommendation for shallow plans rests on multiple independent foundations, not one — which is unusual scope-honesty for a structural claim.
