## Principles Observations

- The strongest names in the corpus are concrete English diagnostics or Greek terms with real conceptual load: `directed separation`, `satisfaction gap`, `control regret`, `adaptive reserve`, `chronica`, `aporia`, `logogenic`, and `logozoetic`.
- Subject-noun-first naming argues against keeping weak terminal nouns such as `pattern`, `framework`, and `principle` when the actual subject is available. `separability ladder`, `coupled diagnostics`, `change proximity`, and `Auftragstaktik bandwidth` are examples.
- The Greek register works best as a stable family vocabulary, not as a universal requirement. TST especially benefits from practical English names because software readers should recognize the operational measurement target immediately.
- Meta-pattern names should become a small memorable family: `separability ladder`, `identifiability floor`, and `forced coordinates`. This family gives the project a shared imagination without turning every segment into metaphor.
- Symbol-heavy slugs should be avoided when the prose already has a good noun. `#disc-m-preservation` is the clearest case; `#disc-model-preservation` carries the same formal content with less reader overhead.
- Names that point to document structure should be demoted when a conceptual name is available. `section-ii-survival` should become `class-2-survival` because the latter survives reorganization.
- Several important quantities need alias discipline more than renaming: `epsilon star` as closure defect, `Delta rho star` as adaptive reserve, `H_b` as backward opacity, `P_hat_Sigma` as plan confidence, and `K_c` as macro-step ratio.
