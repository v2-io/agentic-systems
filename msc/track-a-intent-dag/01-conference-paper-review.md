## Review: Xu & Mohan et al., "AT-Field: Rethinking the Games in Adversarial Training" (AAAI 2026)

**What it does.** AT-Field reframes adversarial training (AT) of neural-network classifiers as a multi-player, non-zero-sum game among individual training samples rather than the standard two-player (attacker vs. defender) zero-sum formulation. The key theoretical contribution is showing that samples within the same mini-batch play an exact potential game (solvable by gradient descent), while samples across different batches can form a non-potential game whose cyclic dynamics prevent convergence. The practical method groups adversarial examples with their most similar clean counterparts via a cosine-similarity cache so that the resulting composite batches restore the potential-game property. This yields modest but consistent improvements in both clean accuracy and adversarial robustness on CIFAR-10/100 and Tiny-ImageNet.

**Relevance to AAD.** The paper is essentially irrelevant to AAD. It addresses a narrow optimization-convergence problem inside the supervised training loop of image classifiers -- there are no agents, goals, feedback dynamics, intent structures, or adaptive cycles. The game-theoretic machinery (potential games, Nash equilibria among samples) is used purely as an analytical lens on gradient-descent convergence, not as a model of purposeful agency or multi-agent coordination. None of its formal results (Theorems 1-3, the potential-function analysis, the sample-matching algorithm) touch on causal DAGs, mismatch-driven control, information-theoretic intent compression, or any other concept in the AAD stack. AAD has no reason to cite or build on this work.
