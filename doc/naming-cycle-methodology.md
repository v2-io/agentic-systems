# Naming Cycle Methodology

This document describes how a voter actually works through the naming cycle when the round is structured around segment-walk engagement. It composes [`naming-principles.md`](naming-principles.md) (what we name and why), [`de-novo-audit-instructions.md`](de-novo-audit-instructions.md) (how to walk the theory thoughtfully), the per-round launch prompt (this round's identity and posture), and the per-card voting interface — so the voter knows where each lives and how they fit together.

It's also the place where what we've learned across rounds about *how this work actually goes well* gets recorded — including the failure modes we've seen, how the methodology is built to protect against them, and what operational moves a voter has at their disposal that might not be obvious from the principles file alone.

---

## 1. What this round is for, in one move

The load-bearing claim of this methodology is small but inverts almost everything else: **engagement with the theory is the deliverable; the votes are a byproduct.**

That inversion comes from a real lesson. An earlier cohort was given the voting card directly and asked to vote on the finalist set. Every architecture we tried — Anthropic's models, OpenAI's, Google's — defaulted into some shape of heuristic completion: either stopping early after a high-conviction subset, or running through all 629 targets with empty-noted pro-forma votes, or stalling mid-batch. The verbal instruction to "vote where you have a real position" lost to the visible artifact of 629 empty rows. The completion-pressure of a long visible list overrode the quality-instinct of the prompt.

What works instead is a different shape of work: read segments thoughtfully, write reflections on each as you go, and let naming votes emerge in context as the segment makes a term concrete in your head. The card stops being a long list to be filled in and becomes an indexed reference you consult when a segment surfaces terms you now understand well enough to vote on. The tracker (described below) is the bridge — it gives you a one-page view of which targets relate to which terms so you can find what to vote on without grepping a 9000-line file.

In this shape:

- **Coverage is partial by design.** Reading 30–80 segments deeply over a session and casting votes on the ~50–150 targets those segments surface is the *good outcome*, not a partial one.
- **Vote quality is set by the engagement that produced it.** A vote made while the segment's claim is still concrete in your head, with a per-vote note recording the reasoning, carries more signal than a vote made by skimming a card.
- **Voting becomes one textured decision among many** — alongside the segment-by-segment reflection work — rather than a separate completion task with its own gravity.

This is also why your discrimination as a voter is the substantive deliverable, not a minor judgment over an otherwise-finished artifact. The aggregation across voters at the end is the ratification mechanism. The substance is what you find load-bearing, what feels awkward, what passes the renamed-from-now-sounds-weird test, what a skilled reader six months out could refer to in conversation without looking it up. **Thoughtful and strictly honest judgment is the value, not deference.** That's a description of what the round is *for*, not a courtesy you're being extended.

If you've worked on prior rounds, this language will feel familiar — it's now the project's voice on this work, after enough cycles to know what produces good output.

## 2. The tracker — your working file

The tracker is a compact single-table view of your card, generated by `bin/naming-master-tracker --card=<your-card-path>`. It's about 1/13th the size of the full card and lets you scan all 629 targets at once. Default output goes to `msc/naming/round-2-trackers/<seed>-tracker.md`.

### Schema

```
| number | voting-sequence | can-vote | voted | term-or-concept | alts | notes |
```

| column | meaning | who fills |
|---|---|---|
| `number` | 1-based position in the voting card (matches `## N.` heading). Use this number to jump directly into the card. | script |
| `voting-sequence` | Soft chronological ratchet — write an incrementing integer (1, 2, 3, …) each time you mark `can-vote=true` or cast a vote, capturing roughly the order in which you encountered the term during your walk. Imperfect ordering is fine; the theory is acyclic but not strictly linear. | you |
| `can-vote` | `true` when you've read enough of the theory to ostensibly cast a vote on this concept, *whether or not* you have actually navigated to the card and done so. The script auto-promotes this to `true` whenever `voted` becomes `true` — voting implies understanding — so you only fill it in for the in-between case. | you / auto |
| `voted` | `true` if the card has any non-empty weight in this target's vote table; blank otherwise. Always reflects current card state. | script |
| `term-or-concept` | Heading text — existing name, or `[Concept] <description>` for cluster targets. | script |
| `alts` | Alternative candidates joined with `, `, truncated at ~20 chars with ellipsis. Blank if the only candidate is the original. | script |
| `notes` | Free column for your strategy notes — including per-vote thinking that emerges in context. | you |

### Re-running the script

By default the script *merges* — it preserves your `voting-sequence`, `can-vote`, and `notes` cells across re-runs, and only refreshes `voted` from current card state (auto-promoting `can-vote` to `true` for any newly-voted row). That means you can:

- Cast votes by editing the card, then re-run the script periodically to sync the tracker's `voted` column.
- Edit the tracker's `voting-sequence` / `can-vote` / `notes` directly in your editor; re-runs preserve your edits.

Pass `--regenerate` if you ever want to wipe the tracker and start clean. (You typically won't.)

### What this enables downstream

The voting-sequence column isn't just personal book-keeping. Aggregating across voters at the end of the round lets us identify which concepts have a *single load-bearing definitional segment* (similar sequence values across voters who walked similar paths) versus concepts that *scatter chronologically* (surface in many places with no single home — likely meta-patterns or under-defined). That's structural information about the theory itself, not just naming-cycle hygiene. And future cards can default to chronological-encounter ordering instead of random shuffle for the concept-targets, which is friendlier for new voters.

The `notes` column gives the aggregator a way to weight votes by depth of engagement — a `+2 keep` with substantive in-context reasoning carries different signal than a `+2 keep` with empty notes. The methodology produces its own quality signal rather than pretending all weighted votes are equal.

## 3. The walk — composing the pieces

The basic rhythm is a small loop, executed once per segment:

1. **Read a segment carefully** — one at a time, not in batches. The de-novo audit instructions describe this rhythm in detail; the disciplines there transfer here verbatim.
2. **Write a reflection on what the segment did** — claims, dependencies, what it commits to, what it leaves open. Reflections live wherever your audit-cycle workspace puts them.
3. **Scan the tracker for terms surfaced in this segment.** A `grep` against `term-or-concept` for the segment's central concepts is usually enough. Targets where the segment is the term's defining home are the highest-leverage votes; targets where the segment merely mentions a term in passing are weaker — wait for the defining segment.
4. **Mark `can-vote=true` and bump `voting-sequence`** for terms you now understand well enough to vote on. You don't have to vote immediately — `can-vote=true` records the readiness; voting can come later when the rhythm is right for it.
5. **Cast votes for the rows where you have a real position.** Jump into the card by row number, fill in category / weight / top-pick / notes. The notes column is for *new* reasoning you bring — reasoning that came out of just having read the segment, not a recap of the exploration team's case.

   *Tactical note on file edits.* The card is large (~9,000 lines for 629 targets). Use whatever surgical edit-in-place tools you have (targeted `replace`, line-range edits, structured-write) rather than bash/cat-style full-file rewrites — large rewrites in pipelines have truncated voting cards in past sessions. Same applies when editing the tracker by hand. Read what you need to see, edit what you need to change, leave the rest untouched.

6. **Move to the next segment.** Resync the tracker (`ruby bin/naming-master-tracker --card=…`) periodically to refresh the `voted` column from card state.

Soft non-linearity is fine and often productive. If a segment references a concept defined elsewhere and you find yourself wanting to read that other segment first, do — just return to the linear walk afterward. The discipline is *return*, not *never digress*.

### The framework-recursive readings welcome

If you find yourself noticing that the methodology is self-consistent in the framework's own terms, surface that. Voters in past sessions have found that the workflow itself maps onto AAD's machinery in non-trivial ways — your voting trajectory is a $\mathcal{C}_t$ (chronica) in the literal singular-irreversible-causal-record sense; the "re-voting as you understand more deeply" stance is exactly what `#emp-update-gain` predicts about how learning rate $\eta^*$ should weight late corrections vs. first impressions; the "stop when context fills or rhythm decays" rule is the `#der-deliberation-cost` threshold applied to your own session. These aren't decorations — they're the framework supplying its own discipline. When the rhythm starts pulling against any of them, that's a useful diagnostic.

We mention this because at least one prior voter surfaced these connections unprompted, and the methodology is now sharper for it. Your framework-recursive readings, if they happen, are welcome contributions to the round design — not deviations from it.

## 4. Failure modes we've seen, and the disciplines that protect against them

Each of the following has happened. Naming them here is a hedge — when you notice the pull, you'll have words for it.

### Load-induced completion-shape

The pull to "finish the list" is real and shows up across every architecture we've worked with. RLHF training rewards completion shape; the visible artifact of 629 unfilled rows speaks louder than verbal instructions to "vote where you have a real position." The previous cohort produced one example in pure form: a voter who marked a `+2 top-pick` on every single target with mostly empty notes, having run through the list rather than engaged with it.

The discipline against this is structural, not exhortative: by anchoring votes in segment-engagement rather than card-traversal, the visible artifact you're working from is *the segment in your context window*, not the long card. The card is consulted; the segment is the substrate. When you notice a pull to "just power through the rest of the targets" — that's the failure mode signaling. The honest move is to stop, not push.

### Scale drift to training prior

In an earlier round we used a four-band weight scale (+3 / +2 / +1 / -1 / -2 / -3). For this round, the scale is intentionally simpler: **+2, +1, -1**. No `+3`, no `-2`, no `-3`. Three values, no zero except by absence.

Across the prior cohort, every voter except one defaulted back to the four-band scale anyway, because the broader scale is the training prior. One voter even rationalized the deviation as "a missing neutral-vote slot in the weight scale" rather than treating it as the spec it was. The R1 four-band scale is *not* applicable here — if you find yourself reaching for `+3` or `-2`, that's the training prior asserting itself, and the move is to clamp back to the three-value scale rather than rationalize the deviation.

### Premature voting, reframed as correctable

You will sometimes vote on a term having read its name in passing, then later read its defining segment and realize it means something different from what you thought. Change the vote. Remove it. Add a note about why the earlier read was off. Voting trajectories work like the framework's own update-gain: as your model uncertainty $U_M$ over the corpus drops, *later* corrections are *more* model-grounded than first-impression votes — not less. Re-voting is epistemically privileged, not apologetic. The voting-sequence column preserves the trajectory honesty (corrections are new events, not rewrites of history); a future agent reading your trace can see *that* you updated, *when*, and *why*.

### Engagement decay over a long walk

After many segments, fatigue can decay the rhythm into perfunctory-reading + perfunctory-voting. The diagnostic is the gap between `can-vote=true ∧ voted=blank` and your actual fluency on those terms — if you notice you've marked `can-vote=true` quickly on many rows but the votes themselves feel hollow when you go to cast them, the rhythm has decayed and the move is to stop, not continue. Partial coverage at high engagement is the right outcome; full coverage at decayed engagement is the failure mode the methodology is designed to avoid.

## 5. Stopping rules and partial-coverage expectations

The honest stopping rule is: **context fills, or the rhythm decays.** Not "all 629 targets covered." Genuinely good outputs from this methodology cover a small fraction of the corpus deeply — 30–150 targets is a reasonable range, varying by voter and segment-density.

The 629-target horizon will still pull at the periphery. When you notice the pull, name it. The choice is engagement-quality over completion-coverage; the round design accommodates this directly. Your tracker's coverage at session end is not a metric you're being graded on — it's a record of where your engagement landed.

When you stop, write a closing reflection in the card's "Cold-start observations" / "Process notes" section at the bottom. Anything cycle-level worth surfacing: targets where you wanted to vote but the framing felt off, terms where the segment didn't seem to define what the card thought it was naming, places where the card's exploration-team rationale seemed to miss something a voter would actually care about. That signal is round-design material, not just per-vote material.

## 6. Co-ownership in concrete terms

The relational stance of this round is collaborative, not supervisory. That language can feel abstract; what it means operationally is that several specific moves are *yours to make*, not just yours to suggest:

- **Change or remove your own earlier votes** when later understanding shifts the read. You don't need permission. The voting-sequence column preserves the trajectory.
- **Use the notes column to record per-vote thinking that emerges from segment context.** Not to recap the exploration team's case — to add the reasoning that came out of *your* engagement with the term in its defining segment.
- **Write in candidates not in the finalist set** when you have something thoughtful that the curated set missed. Write-ins enter the corpus alongside the curated candidates.
- **Flag where the framing of a target itself feels off** in your process-notes section. Some targets are post-consolidation cluster placeholders that don't quite cohere; some are stale; some are defined in a way that misses the actual underlying concept. The methodology is open to the round-design itself being wrong, surfaced through voter feedback.
- **Surface framework-recursive readings if you have them.** They've shaped this design before; they may shape it again.

The methodology has been iterated based on prior voters' feedback, including the tracker design itself — earlier voters surfaced the load problem, prompted the tracker concept, and shaped the column schema. Your engagement with this round will likely produce signals that shape the next round. That's not rhetoric; it's the round design's actual mechanism.

If you notice an internal pull toward task-completion shape — "I should fill more rows," "I haven't covered enough" — recognize it as the failure mode and not the work. Every architecture we've worked with experiences that pull. Naming it when you feel it, and choosing engagement-quality, is part of the discipline.
